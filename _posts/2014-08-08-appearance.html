I worked on my last D3.js project two to three weeks ago, and put it up on bl.ocks here with code also on github here.

Between that time and now, I had an two rounds of interviews with Hacker School in New York, and was not accepted. It was a long-shot, being a total novice to "programming" (quotes either because of impostor syndrome or because I am actually a programming impostor!). The first interview was a cinch, with about 15 minutes of conversation on Skype with a facilitator there. The second interview was so nerve-wracking for me. It basically took over everything else I was doing, between my many domestic responsibilities and any other projects I might have begun after the SF Bay Area choropleth.

And here's why: it was a pair programming interview. Me?! Pair programming?! That was wild. I had never programmed professionally, and here I was going over my code with someone who's really good at this stuff? I decided to refactor a program I had written in Haskell (here), which creates basically a choropleth map, but I had done it last November from the ground-up, linking SVG coordinates to points to create lines, including Bezier curves, and several other complications. I decided to refactor that code instead of working on a D3.js project, because the instructions specifically said not to work on something that involved a framework.

I hadn't looked at Haskell or my code since last December, and suddenly I had to remember everything I had done, and I had left myself about two days to do it. I got as prepared as I could; the interview proved that I really am not prepared for pair programming. I have gotten to where I am through sheer force of will, and without having very good fundamentals. The interview was quite embarrassing for me. BUT I do have to pat myself on the back for going through with 45 minutes of skype pair programming with someone who really knows their stuff. It's like I crossed some kind of weird line that suddenly showed up out of nowhere and dared me to cross it.

It turns out, there's nothing on the other side but the experience had. Not totally true, though. Being forced to talk about something so awkwardly with someone for so long because I didn't really know the answers to questions I was being asked but it was kind of assumed I would, has made interactions with strangers way less daunting. Also, the experience forced me to realize how lacking in fundamentals I am.

Since then, I have completed four weeks of the free CS101 online course with Stanford, and realizing this might not be rigorous enough, I've signed up for another class on object-oriented programming that will be for credit.
